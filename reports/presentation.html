What's This All About?

If you've ever waited at a train station wondering "why is it always late?" — this project tries to answer that question with data.

🔍

The Problem

Deutsche Bahn trains are often delayed. Weather conditions (rain, snow, wind) and other factors play a big role. Can we predict delays before they happen?

🎯

The Goal

Build a computer system that looks at schedule info and weather data, then predicts: "Will this train be late?" and "How many minutes?"

💡

How We Built It: CRISP-DM

Think of this like a recipe. We followed a 6-step process that data scientists use worldwide to solve real problems.

1. Business Understanding

We defined the goal: predict delays so passengers can plan better and DB can operate more efficiently.

2. Data Understanding

We gathered train schedules for 15 major German stations (50,000 records) and fetched weather data from the Open-Meteo API (315,000 hourly measurements). Then we explored the data to find patterns.

3. Data Preparation

We merged train and weather data, cleaned it up, and created 21 useful features. For example: "Is it rush hour?", "What season is it?", "How much did the previous train get delayed?"

4. Modeling

We trained 14 different ML models (7 for classification, 7 for regression). Each model learned from the data to make predictions.

5. Evaluation

We tested every model and ranked them. The best classifier was Logistic Regression (63% AUC). The best regressor was Linear Regression (RMSE = 3.8 minutes).

6. Deployment

We saved the best models, created 27 charts and graphs, and produced this report — all with a single command!

💡 Non-technical summary: We collected train and weather data, taught computers to find patterns in it, tested which computer program worked best, and made everything automatic.

Our Data Story

Every good prediction starts with good data. Here's what we collected and where it came from.

Source 1
🚆

Train Schedules

50,000 records from 15 major German stations including Berlin Hbf, München Hbf, Hamburg Hbf, Frankfurt Hbf, and more. We have scheduled vs actual arrival times, train types (ICE, IC, RE, RB, S-Bahn), and delay minutes.

Source 2
🌦️

Weather Data

315,000 hourly weather measurements from the Open-Meteo API (a free weather service). We pulled temperature, rain, snowfall, humidity, cloud cover, pressure, wind speed, and visibility for every station.

The Merge
🤝

Joined Together

We combined both datasets using a simple key: Station + Date + Hour. So every train arrival is matched with the weather at that station, on that day, at that hour. 60% of records found a perfect weather match.

📊 Delay vs On-Time: About half the trains (49%) were delayed more than 5 minutes. The other half (51%) were on time.
📊 How delays are spread out: Most delays are short (1-5 minutes). Long delays (15+ minutes) are rare. This is called a "right-skewed" distribution.

Key Insights & Patterns

Before building any models, we explored the data to find interesting patterns. Here's what we discovered.

🕒 When Are Delays Worst?

🕒 By Hour: Delays peak during evening rush hour (5-7 PM). Morning commutes also see higher delays.
📅 By Weekday: Fridays have the highest delays — everyone is trying to go home for the weekend!
🏠 By Month: December and January (winter) have the worst delays. Summer (July-August) is more reliable.
❄️ By Season: Winter is the worst season for delays. Spring and summer are better for travel.

🚆 Which Trains Are Most Delayed?

🚆 Train Type Analysis: Regional trains (RB, RE) tend to be delayed more often than high-speed ICE/IC trains. S-Bahn (city trains) perform best.

🌦️ Does Weather Affect Delays?

Yes! We found clear relationships between weather and train delays. Scroll through the charts below.

🌡️ Temperature: More delays in very cold weather (below 0°C) and extreme heat.
🌧️ Rain: More rain = more delays. Wet tracks and reduced visibility slow trains down.
❄️ Snow: Even a little snow causes delays. Heavy snow can cause major disruptions.
💨 Wind: Strong winds (40+ km/h) are associated with longer delays, especially for high-speed trains.

The Features We Used

Features are like clues the computer uses to make a prediction. Here are all 21 clues we gave each model.

🕒 8 features

Time-Based Clues

Hour of day (rush hour vs quiet night), day of week (Monday vs Sunday), month, season, whether it's a weekend, rush hour, morning peak, or evening peak.

🌦️ 8 features

Weather Clues

Temperature, rainfall, snowfall, humidity, cloud cover, pressure, wind speed, and a weather severity score (0 = clear skies, 4 = thunderstorm).

🔄 2 features

History Clues

Previous delay: Was the last train at this station delayed? Rolling average: What's the average delay over the last 3 trains?

🎯 3 encoded

Category Clues

Train type (ICE, IC, RE, RB, etc.) and station name are converted into numerical form that the computer can understand.

Conclusions & Key Takeaways

What we learned from this project, what worked, what didn't, and what could be done better.

✅ Finding 1
🌡️

Weather Matters, But Isn't Everything

Temperature, rain, snow, and wind all correlate with delays. However, our models could only explain about 6.5% of delay variation using weather + schedule data. The remaining ~94% comes from other factors: track maintenance, staffing shortages, signal issues, construction work, and connecting train waits.

✅ Finding 2
🕒

Timing Is Critical

Delays follow clear temporal patterns: Fridays are worst, winter is hardest, and evening rush hour (5-7 PM) peaks. Regional trains (RB/RE) suffer more than high-speed ICE/IC trains. This suggests infrastructure quality and train priority systems play a big role.

✅ Finding 3
🎯

Simple Models Compete With Complex Ones

Logistic Regression (simplest classifier) tied or beat Neural Networks and Gradient Boosting. Linear Regression won for regression. This tells us: for this problem, simple, interpretable models are just as good as black-box complex ones. No need for deep learning here!

✅ Finding 4
⚠️

Prediction Is Genuinely Hard

60% accuracy and 3.8-minute RMSE are decent baselines but not production-ready. Delay prediction is a notoriously difficult problem worldwide, not just for Deutsche Bahn. The stochastic nature of rail operations (a single signal failure cascades) makes perfect prediction impossible.

✅ Finding 5
📦

Data Quality Is Key

With only 60% weather match rate and synthetic train data (since real DB data isn't publicly available), our results are proof-of-concept. Real operational data would dramatically improve performance — especially real-time delay tracking and infrastructure status feeds.

✅ Finding 6
🧠

Feature Engineering Helped

Adding lag delay (previous train's delay) and rolling mean delay improved model performance. This shows that delay history is one of the strongest predictors — if the last train was late, the next one probably will be too (due to shared infrastructure).

Simple Glossary

Technical terms explained in plain English.

Machine LearningA computer learning patterns from data instead of being explicitly programmed. Like showing a child many photos of cats until they can recognize one.
ClassificationPredicting a category. Like spam detection: "Is this email spam?" (Yes/No). We predict: "Is this train delayed?"
RegressionPredicting a number. Like a house price estimate. We predict: "How many minutes late?"
AccuracyThe percentage of predictions the model got right. 60% accuracy = correct 6 out of 10 times.
ROC AUCA score from 0.5 to 1.0 measuring overall prediction quality. 0.5 = random guessing, 1.0 = perfect predictions.
RMSEAverage prediction error in minutes (or original units). RMSE = 3.8 means predictions are typically off by about 3.8 minutes.
FeatureA piece of information given to the model. Like a clue in a detective case. Examples: temperature, hour of day, train type.
CRISP-DMA 6-step process data scientists follow to solve problems. Like a recipe for data projects.
One-Hot EncodingConverting categories (like "ICE" or "RB") into numbers the computer can understand.
Open-Meteo APIA free online service that provides historical weather data for any location on Earth.
